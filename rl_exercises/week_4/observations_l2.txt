L2 Observations — RLiable for Seeded Extended DQN
=================================================

NOTE:
- for l2 extended_dqn is just regular dqn without the bells and whistles.
- figures:
  - ./plots/l2/*.png

Setup
-----
Environment  : CartPole-v1
Agent        : extended_dqn
Seeds        : 10
Evaluation   : final metric taken from the last recorded eval reward per seed
Reporting    : RLiable sample-efficiency curve and final metrics (IQM, median, mean, optimality gap)

Observed final scores across seeds were:
110, 120, 137, 145, 154, 159, 177, 177, 233, 500

This is already enough to show that the seed distribution is skewed: one seed solved
the environment with a final score of 500, while most seeds finished much lower, in
roughly the 110-233 range.

What changes when using RLiable vs. plain averages?
---------------------------------------------------
Using plain averages would summarize the seeded runs with one number and one average
curve. That hides how uneven the seed distribution actually is. In these results, the
plain final mean is 191.2, which is noticeably higher than what most individual seeds
achieved. The reason is that the single 500-score run pulls the average upward.

RLiable changes the interpretation in two ways:

1. It uses robust aggregate statistics such as IQM and median, not only the mean.
   These are less sensitive to one unusually strong seed.

2. It shows bootstrap confidence intervals, so the plots communicate uncertainty across
   seeds instead of presenting the aggregate as if it were exact.

For these runs, the important qualitative result is that the mean looks more optimistic
than the robust summaries. That suggests the "typical" seed performance is weaker than
the plain average implies. In other words, the average overstates how reliable the agent
is, while the RLiable plots expose that the good result is not representative of most
seeds.

Do I feel more confident in the results? Why or why not?
--------------------------------------------------------
I feel more confident in the interpretation, but not necessarily more confident that the
agent is strong. The RLiable figures make it clearer what is actually happening:

- the sample-efficiency curve summarizes learning across seeds rather than showing only
  one lucky trajectory;
- the final-metric plot shows that there is a large gap between the optimistic mean and
  the more robust summaries;
- the bootstrap intervals make the uncertainty visible instead of hiding it.

So RLiable increases confidence in the *reporting*, because it reveals that performance
is seed-sensitive and that one outlier seed is inflating the mean. At the same time, it
reduces confidence in a simple claim like "extended_dqn reliably solves CartPole",
because the seeded results do not support that conclusion. Most seeds do not end near
the solved score, and the optimality-gap plot confirms that the method remains far from
consistently optimal across seeds.

Conclusion
----------
Compared with plain averages, the RLiable figures give a more honest summary of seeded
performance. They show that extended_dqn can occasionally perform extremely well, but
its typical performance is lower and less consistent than the mean alone suggests. That
makes the results more trustworthy as an analysis, even though it also shows that the
agent is less reliable than a plain average would imply.
