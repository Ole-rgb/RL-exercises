L3 Observations — exp2 Comparison (Baseline vs Prioritized Replay vs Double DQN)
=================================================================================

Setup
-----
Experiment 2 compares four variants of `extended_dqn` on `CartPole-v1` with 3 seeds
(`0`, `1`, `10`):

- baseline = normal DQN + uniform replay
- prioritized replay = normal DQN + prioritized replay
- double dqn = Double DQN + uniform replay
- double dqn + prioritized = Double DQN + prioritized replay

The RLiable figures for this experiment are:

- `exp2_sample_efficiency.png`
- `exp2_final_metrics.png`

What the figures show
---------------------
The strongest result is that prioritized replay is clearly the best-performing variant.
Its final evaluation scores are `259`, `500`, and `316`, which are all above or equal
to the corresponding baseline and Double DQN results. In the sample-efficiency plot, it
also learns faster and stays above the other variants for most of training.

Double DQN alone gives a small improvement over the baseline. Its final scores
(`212`, `155`, `210`) are slightly better on average than baseline
(`156`, `198`, `192`), and the final normalized mean/IQM/median moves from about
`0.364` to `0.385`. This is a real but fairly modest gain.

The combined variant `double dqn + prioritized` performs worst. Its final scores
(`162`, `152`, `95`) are lower than all other variants, and its optimality gap is the
largest. So in this small experiment, combining the two modifications does not help;
instead it appears to destabilize learning.

How to interpret the RLiable metrics here
-----------------------------------------
With only 3 seeds per variant, the reported IQM, median, and mean are numerically the
same for all four methods. That is not a bug in the plotting. It happens because with
so few samples, the robust summaries collapse to nearly the same statistic. This means
the final-metric plot is still useful for ranking methods, but it is less informative
about distribution shape than it would be with more seeds.

Even with that limitation, the ordering is very clear:

- prioritized replay: best
- double dqn: second
- baseline: third
- double dqn + prioritized: worst

The optimality-gap panel tells the same story from the opposite direction. Prioritized
replay has the smallest gap to the normalized optimum, while `double dqn + prioritized`
has the largest.

Confidence in the results
-------------------------
I am more confident in the direction of the ranking than I would be from a single-seed
comparison, because all methods were evaluated across multiple seeds and the same
qualitative conclusion appears in both the sample-efficiency and final-metric plots.

However, confidence is still limited because the comparison only uses 3 seeds. That is
enough to see a strong difference when one method is consistently better, as prioritized
replay is here, but it is not enough to make subtle claims about smaller differences or
about the exact uncertainty. In particular, the equality of mean, median, and IQM in
this setting should be interpreted as a consequence of low sample count, not as evidence
that the seed distributions are perfectly symmetric.

Conclusion
----------
For `exp2`, prioritized replay is the clear winner. It improves both sample efficiency
and final performance relative to the baseline. Double DQN by itself helps a little, but
the gain is much smaller than the gain from prioritized replay. Combining Double DQN
with prioritized replay performs surprisingly badly in these runs, so the two extensions
do not appear complementary under the current hyperparameter settings.
