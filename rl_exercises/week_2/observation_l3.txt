Level 3 observations

The contextual Mars Rover experiments compared contextual and non-contextual planning agents across three scenario families:
tilt-only variation, friction-only variation, and joint tilt-and-friction variation.

In the single-factor settings, context was useful.
Contextual value iteration performed best in both tilt-only and friction-only experiments,
and contextual policy iteration also improved over the corresponding non-contextual policy baseline. 
This suggests that when the source of variation is simple and structured, explicit context can help the planner choose better actions.

The joint variation setting was harder. In that case, both non-contextual baselines outperformed the contextual
agents on the final test split. The per-context results show that the contextual agents did not fail uniformly;
instead, they handled some held-out contexts well and others very poorly,
especially harder combinations with stronger friction. This means the joint contextual setting is
sensitive to the exact train/test context split and to evaluation variance.

Overall, the experiments show that context can help, but not automatically.
In this small tabular setup, contextual value iteration was the strongest contextual method,
while the non-contextual baselines remained surprisingly competitive, and even better, in the joint setting.
